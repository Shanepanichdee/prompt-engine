# ChatGPT Prompts for HR Managers: 5 Frameworks That Actually Work

Most HR managers use AI the same way they use Google: type a sentence, press enter, hope for the best.

"Write a job description for a senior developer."

What comes back is technically a job description. It covers the right categories. It uses the right words. And it looks exactly like the job description every other company posted last week.

The problem isn't ChatGPT. The problem is the prompt. When you give the AI no context, no audience, no constraints — it produces the most average possible output. That's not a bug. That's what "no context" produces.

Structured prompts fix this. Instead of one vague sentence, you give the AI the five things it needs to produce genuinely useful output: who it should be, what you want, who's reading it, how to write it, and what format to use.

This guide covers five frameworks specifically useful for HR work — with real examples for each.

---

## Why Structured Prompts Produce Better Output

A prompt framework is a form. Instead of leaving the AI to guess what you mean, you fill in specific fields that define exactly what the output should be.

The best frameworks were developed by professional prompt engineers and AI researchers who noticed that certain types of structure consistently produce better results. They're not magic — they're checklists that force specificity before the AI writes anything.

For HR work, the payoff is concrete: job descriptions that reflect your actual culture, interview questions tailored to your hiring criteria, SOPs that a new employee can actually follow.

---

## Framework 1: CO-STAR — For Job Descriptions and Communication

CO-STAR controls six dimensions of AI output simultaneously: **C**ontext, **O**bjective, **S**tyle, **T**one, **A**udience, and **R**esponse.

It's particularly powerful for anything that needs to reach a specific audience with a specific tone — which describes most HR writing.

**Example: Senior Backend Engineer Job Description**

| Field | Input |
|-------|-------|
| Context | Fast-growing B2B SaaS startup, 40-person engineering team, remote-first |
| Objective | Write a compelling job description for a Senior Backend Engineer |
| Style | Professional but human — candidates should feel excited, not intimidated |
| Tone | Direct, inclusive, encouraging |
| Audience | Experienced engineers with 5+ years, likely browsing multiple listings |
| Response | 400 words max. Open with why this role matters. Bullet responsibilities and requirements separately. |

What makes this work: the AI knows the company culture, the competitive context (candidates comparing listings), and exactly how to format the output. The Style and Tone fields alone separate CO-STAR from any other framework — they produce a voice, not just content.

**When to use CO-STAR:** Job descriptions, offer letters, all-hands communications, policy announcements to employees.

## Framework 2: RISEN — For Interview Questions and Process Documents

RISEN is built for tasks that follow a sequence: **R**ole, **I**nstructions, **S**teps, **E**nd Goal, **N**arrowing.

The Steps field is the differentiator. When you need the AI to follow a specific process — not just produce content, but follow a structure — RISEN keeps it on track.

**Example: Behavioral Interview Questions for Culture Fit**

| Field | Input |
|-------|-------|
| Role | Experienced HR interviewer specializing in behavioral assessment |
| Instructions | Create 10 behavioral interview questions for a mid-level product manager role |
| Steps | Focus on past behavior, use STAR format, probe collaboration and decision-making under ambiguity |
| End Goal | Questions that reveal culture fit and problem-solving style — not just qualifications |
| Narrowing | No trick questions. Inclusive language. Avoid questions that disadvantage caregivers. |

The Narrowing field is what separates RISEN from a basic prompt. It eliminates what you don't want — which is often harder to specify than what you do want.

**When to use RISEN:** Interview question banks, SOPs, onboarding checklists, any process with multiple defined steps.

## Framework 3: RTCF — For Quick Tasks

RTCF (Role, Task, Context, Format) is the fastest framework to fill and the right starting point for most everyday HR requests.

Four fields. Sixty seconds. Output that's actually usable.

**Example: 30-Day Onboarding Checklist**

| Field | Input |
|-------|-------|
| Role | HR operations manager at a mid-size tech company |
| Task | Create a 30-day onboarding checklist for a new remote software engineer |
| Context | For a company with no existing onboarding documentation. Engineer starts Monday. Manager is in a different timezone. |
| Format | Numbered list, grouped by week. Include a "responsible party" column for each item (HR, Manager, or New Hire). |

The Context field is what prevents generic output. Without it, the AI doesn't know if this is for a 10-person startup or a 10,000-person enterprise, whether it's in-person or remote, or whether documentation already exists.

**When to use RTCF:** Quick policy drafts, meeting agendas, email templates, checklist creation, one-off requests where you know exactly what you need.

## Framework 4: CRISPE — For Complex HR Policy Documents

When the stakes are high and the output needs to be authoritative, CRISPE provides the most complete structure: **C**apacity, **R**ole, **I**nsight, **S**tatement, **P**ersonality, **E**xperiment.

The Capacity and Personality fields are what make CRISPE distinctive. Capacity specifies what expertise the AI should bring; Personality specifies how it should communicate. Together they produce a voice that's distinctive, not generic.

**Example: Disciplinary Action Policy**

| Field | Input |
|-------|-------|
| Capacity | Senior HR attorney with 15 years of employment law and policy writing experience |
| Role | HR policy writer for a 500-person company in the technology sector |
| Insight | The company operates in California — state law creates specific requirements around progressive discipline documentation |
| Statement | Write a disciplinary action policy covering verbal warnings, written warnings, performance improvement plans, and termination |
| Personality | Precise and legally careful — plain language where possible, but no ambiguity on process or rights |
| Experiment | Flag any section where California-specific requirements differ from federal baseline |

The Experiment field is a hidden power: you can ask the AI to surface its own uncertainties, flag edge cases, or suggest alternatives. For policy documents, this catches gaps before they become problems.

**When to use CRISPE:** Policy documents, compliance materials, high-stakes communications, anything that needs legal or domain-expert accuracy.

## Framework 5: Chain of Thought — For Difficult Conversations

Difficult conversation preparation is where most HR professionals feel least supported by AI tools. The standard advice ("be empathetic, be direct") doesn't help you write the actual words.

Chain of Thought prompting changes this by asking the AI to reason through the situation before producing output.

**Example: Termination Conversation Script**

| Field | Input |
|-------|-------|
| Task | Prepare a conversation script for a manager delivering a termination for performance after a failed 60-day PIP |
| Reasoning style | Work through each phase of the conversation: opening, explanation, logistics, employee response scenarios, close. Consider what the employee is likely to feel at each stage before writing the script. |
| Output format | Scene-by-scene script with: manager's words, likely employee responses (3 scenarios), and coaching notes for the manager |

The reasoning step — "consider what the employee is likely to feel at each stage before writing" — changes the output fundamentally. Instead of a script that sounds right to the manager, you get one that accounts for how the conversation will actually land.

**When to use Chain of Thought:** Difficult conversation preparation, complex investigation summaries, policy exception decisions, any situation where you need the AI to think through implications before answering.
